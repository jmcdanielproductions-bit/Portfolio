Project
Overview

As one of the biggest Cyberpunk fans on the team, I approached this wanting to stay faithful to the tone and identity of the world — not create another product advertisement. The goal was to make something fans would immediately recognize as authentic.

Challenge

Cyberpunk's audience knows the world immediately. The challenge wasn't explaining it — it was capturing the feeling. The piece needed to communicate Arasaka, power, control, danger, and technological paranoia while functioning as a commercial campaign film.

Creative Approach

I directed through Arasaka's identity — not Night City as a whole. Arasaka is about power, control, and menace. The visual direction followed that logic: aggressive red and black palettes, shadow, glitch artifacts, synthetic imperfections, and dense atmosphere. The direction leaned into friction rather than resolving it.

Production

Building Night City

The world was built through practical atmosphere first: lighting, haze, red/green separation, dark surfaces, and controlled production design. The goal was not to create a clean product setup, but to make the frame feel like it already belonged inside Arasaka's world.

Execution
01

Pacing

Arasaka isn't calm. The edit was built around pressure, escalation, and speed that feels like it might break at any moment — but never does. The chaos was intentional.

02

Visual Language

Aggressive red and black palettes, shadow, minimal blue buried deep in the frame. Glitch artifacts and synthetic imperfections reinforce technology that doesn't quite trust itself.

03

Atmosphere

Practical fog created density, separation, and the layered industrial atmosphere that belongs to that world. The goal was a world that felt inhabited, polluted, and slightly unstable.

04

Music

"Resist and Disorder" — the tension between the rebellion in the track and the authority Arasaka represents felt true to Cyberpunk's core conflict. The music doesn't support the visuals. It argues with them.

Direction

Through The Monitor

The monitor became the place where the world either worked or fell apart. Every product shot had to carry the same red-black tension as the performance footage — lighting, reflections, haze, and background texture treated as part of the storytelling, not the setup.
