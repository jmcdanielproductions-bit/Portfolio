This project was created for Bethesda's Fallout Nuka-Cola collection. The brief wasn't to advertise a keyboard, desk mat, Stream Deck, and microphone. The brief was to make a real Nuka-Cola commercial — a lost retro-futuristic advertisement recovered from another timeline. Cheerful, strange, nostalgic, and slightly unsettling.

Challenge

Fallout has one of the most specific tonal signatures in gaming. The challenge wasn't intensity or atmosphere — it was capturing the contradiction that makes the franchise unforgettable: bright 1950s optimism layered over corporate propaganda, post-apocalyptic darkness, and genuinely dark humor. The piece needed to feel like a real Nuka-Cola advertisement while remaining strange enough to belong to that world.

The Concept

The concept came down to a single question: what would a Nuka-Cola commercial look like if the company actually existed? Not a parody — the commercial itself. A piece of in-universe media recovered from the Fallout timeline, broadcast-ready and slightly wrong in ways you can't quite name.

Creative Approach

The creative direction started with one rule: play it completely straight. The humor couldn't come from winking at the audience — it had to come from total sincerity. The actress was directed to feel like she stepped out of a 1950s television commercial: upbeat energy, polished presentation, the slightly eerie optimism of someone who completely believes in what they're selling. Visual references were Fallout's own in-game media, Nuka-Cola branding, vintage Coca-Cola advertising, and Atomic Age Americana. The moment the production breaks character, the concept collapses. You either commit fully or it doesn't work.

Performance

Direction focused on cheerful, sincere, and slightly too perfect — the kind of commercial host who believes everything she's saying. No irony, no self-awareness. The performance needed to land as genuine advertising from inside the Fallout world, not as an impression of it. That distinction is everything.

Visual Language

The opening was shot in black-and-white broadcast style to establish the feeling of a recovered old commercial. A crank-up transition sound bridges the black-and-white section into full color. That cut is the moment the audience understands the concept — this is an in-universe advertisement, not a product showcase. Everything before it is setup; everything after is payoff.

Worldbuilding

Gameplay footage and live-action footage were blended through in-game television-style transitions, connecting the campaign to the Fallout world rather than keeping it separate. Nuka-Cola visual language — brand elements, in-universe ad aesthetics, Bottle and Cappy-style references — helped the live-action material feel like it already existed inside the game.

Sound & Tone

Old-style broadcast music and sound effects reinforced the feeling of a recovered advertisement. The piece leans into Fallout's particular brand of weirdness: nostalgic, charming, commercial, and slightly creepy simultaneously. The tone never breaks. That consistency is what separates a piece that feels authentic from one that just references the source material.

Creative Anchor

The black-and-white commercial opening transitioning into the full-color Fallout campaign is the moment the piece earns its concept. Before the cut, it's a period commercial. After the cut, it's Fallout. That transition is the whole argument in two seconds.

Outcome

The project translated the identity of Nuka-Cola into a real-world campaign while staying faithful to the tone Fallout fans recognize immediately. It demonstrated how humor, nostalgia, gameplay integration, commercial performance direction, and retro visual language can work together to create something that feels like a natural extension of an existing world — not a product placed inside one.
