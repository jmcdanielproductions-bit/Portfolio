Challenge

Project

Overview

The concept came down to one question: what would a Nuka-Cola commercial look like if the company actually existed? Not a parody — the commercial itself. A piece of in-universe media recovered from the Fallout timeline, broadcast-ready and slightly wrong in ways you can't quite name.

Director's note 01

The commercial cannot know the world ended. That is the whole joke, and the whole reason it works.

Challenge

Fallout's tonal signature is a precise balance: bright 1950s optimism layered over dark humor and post-apocalyptic weirdness. Lean too sincere and it becomes a history lesson. Lean too winking and it becomes a parody. The challenge is finding the frequency where both are simultaneously true.

Director's note 02

If the tone winked even once, the spot would become cosplay. Sincerity was the discipline.

Creative Approach

The creative direction started with one rule: play it completely straight. No winking at the audience. No acknowledging the fiction. The humor comes entirely from context — the audience knows what this world is, the commercial doesn't. That gap is where all the comedy lives. The moment the direction breaks character, the concept collapses.

Director's note 03

We played the sales language straight and let the Fallout context corrupt it from underneath.

Execution

01

Performance

Directed toward cheerful, sincere, and slightly too perfect — the 1950s commercial host who completely believes everything she's saying. No irony, no self-awareness. The performance lands as genuine advertising from inside the Fallout world, not an impression of it.

Director's note 04

The performance direction was "too perfect, never creepy." The creepiness belongs to the world, not the actor.

02

Visual Language

The opening was shot in black-and-white broadcast style to establish the feeling of a recovered old commercial. A crank-up transition sound bridges into full color. That cut is the moment the audience understands the concept — this is an in-universe advertisement.

Director's note 05

The black-and-white opening is a contract. The color shift is when the viewer accepts the broadcast artifact.

03

Worldbuilding

Gameplay footage and live-action were blended through in-game television-style transitions, connecting the campaign to the Fallout world rather than keeping it separate. Nuka-Cola visual language helped the live-action material feel like it already existed inside the game.

Director's note 06

Gameplay was treated like station footage, not cutaway content. It had to feel broadcast through the same universe.

04

Tone & Sound

Old-style broadcast music and sound effects reinforced the feeling of a recovered advertisement. The piece leans into Fallout's particular brand of weirdness: nostalgic, charming, commercial, and slightly creepy — simultaneously. The tone never breaks.

Director's note 07

The sound design does the time travel. A crank, a cue, a little broadcast dirt, and the spot changes century.

Outcome

The project translated the identity of Nuka-Cola into a real-world campaign while staying faithful to the tone Fallout fans recognize immediately. It demonstrated how humor, nostalgia, gameplay integration, and commercial performance direction can work together to create something that feels like a natural extension of an existing world — not a product placed inside one.

Director's note 08

The products are everywhere, but they never plead for attention. They are props inside a believable ad.

Process

Director's note 09

The reference pass was not "Fallout screenshots." It was ad grammar: posture, rhythm, smiles, wipes, sales logic.
